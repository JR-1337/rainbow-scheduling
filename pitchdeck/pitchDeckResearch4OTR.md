## 2. The real cost of pen-and-paper scheduling (Slide 2)

### Manager time consumed by manual scheduling

Retail managers spend roughly **20% of their working time — approximately 8 hours per week** — on manual scheduling tasks. A Pebb.io workforce management analysis found **56% of managers spend 3 to over 10 hours weekly** creating and adjusting schedules. At a manager salary of ~$55,000 CAD/year (~$26.44/hr), that translates to approximately **$11,000 CAD/year** consumed purely by scheduling administration. Named case studies quantify the problem further: **Schuler Shoes**, a 100-year-old retailer with 9 locations and 250 employees, had 12 managers each spending **15–20 hours per week** on Excel-based scheduling before switching to digital tools, which cut that time in half. **Turney's Ace Hardware** (30 employees, Ohio) reduced payroll-prep time from **4 hours to under 1 hour per week**. Industry-wide, automated scheduling reduces scheduling-related activities by **up to 83%** per store and human-made scheduling mistakes by **95%**.

### Dollar cost of scheduling errors

A peer-reviewed study by **Mani, Kesavan & Swaminathan (2015)**, published in *Production and Operations Management*, analyzed 41 stores of a specialty apparel retailer and found all stores were **systematically understaffed during 3-hour peak periods**. Aligning staffing with traffic patterns could yield a **6.15% recovery in lost sales** and **5.74% improvement in profitability**. MIT Sloan/Harvard Business Review research shows retailers lose approximately **4% of revenue for every 1% reduction in payroll hours** below optimal levels. A **2025 Logile survey** found **77% of frontline retail workers** say their store loses sales due to poor scheduling, and **73%** have watched customers walk out due to insufficient staff. On the overstaffing side, the same Mani et al. study found overstaffing **reduces profitability by approximately 2%**, and XShift AI calculated that scheduling just 2 extra employees per shift at $15/hr across 5 shifts/week adds up to **over $46,000/year** in wasted labor.

**Applied to a 40-person store with ~$2M annual revenue:**
- Understaffing losses (4% of revenue): **~$80,000 CAD/year** in missed sales
- Overstaffing waste (conservative): **~$25,000–$46,000 CAD/year**
- Combined scheduling-error exposure: **$105,000–$126,000 CAD/year**

### Turnover driven by scheduling dissatisfaction

**The Shift Project** (Harvard Kennedy School, Schneider & Harknett, 2019) — a survey of **30,000+ employees at 120 of the largest U.S. retail and food-service firms** — provides the definitive data:
- **75% of workers** prefer a more stable, predictable schedule
- **80% of workers** have little to no input into their schedules
- Workers receiving **less than 72 hours' advance notice had a 39% six-month turnover rate** versus 24% for those receiving 2+ weeks' notice
- Workers experiencing **cancelled shifts had a 42% turnover rate**

A 2023 study published in *Manufacturing & Service Operations Management* (Bergman, David & Song) found that **30 days of high schedule volatility during a year increases a worker's probability of quitting by 20%**. McKinsey reports frontline retail workers face annual turnover rates of **at least 60%**, with inconsistent scheduling as a significant contributor. The **National Retail Federation** found that retailers offering scheduling flexibility saw a **15% reduction in turnover**. **35% of workers aged 18–25** left their last hourly job specifically due to inconsistent schedules — more than double the rate of older workers.

### Cost to replace a retail employee in Canada

Replacing a single retail employee in Canada costs between **$3,500 and $5,000 CAD** when accounting for recruiting, training, and lost productivity during ramp-up (SHRM). The **Center for American Progress** pegs the replacement cost for low-wage, high-turnover positions at **16% of annual salary** (~$3,328 for a $10/hr employee). **SHRM's broader benchmark** is **6 to 9 months of salary**. Canadian-specific data from Enkel.ca shows onboarding alone costs **$1,000–$5,000 per hire**, and new hires take **5–6 months** to reach full productivity.

**Applied to Over the Rainbow:** At 60% industry-average turnover on 40 employees = **24 departures/year**. At $4,000 average replacement cost = **$96,000 CAD/year** in turnover costs. Even a modest **5% turnover reduction** saves $4,800/year.

## 3. Ontario ESA compliance — real penalties and cases (Slide 7)

### Current fines and penalties

Ontario's penalty framework operates on three escalating tiers. **Administrative penalties** (Notices of Contravention) start at **$250 per affected employee** for first violations, rise to $500 for second violations within 3 years, and reach **$5,000 per affected employee** for third-plus contraventions — this ceiling was increased fivefold in May 2024 via O. Reg. 189/24. For a retailer with 40 employees, a third contravention affecting the full staff could mean a **$200,000 penalty** in a single notice.

**Part I tickets** (Provincial Offences Act) carry set fines of approximately **$355–$360 per ticket**. **Part III prosecutions** for serious offences hit significantly harder: individuals face fines up to **$100,000** (doubled from $50,000 by Bill 190, Working for Workers Five Act, October 2024) plus up to **12 months imprisonment**. Corporations face up to **$100,000 for first conviction**, **$250,000 for second**, and **$500,000 for subsequent convictions**. Failure to comply with court orders compounds at **$2,000/day** (individual) or **$4,000/day** (corporation) — accumulating to **$124,000+** over a single month. Ontario's Minister of Labour, David Piccini, stated in May 2024: *"We are sending a clear message to those bad actor employers out there that there are real consequences for bad behaviour."*

**Enforcement is accelerating sharply.** ESA claim investigations rose to **11,940 in fiscal 2024–25** — the highest in five years. Total prosecutions have nearly **tripled** from 40 (2022–23) to **111** (2024–25). The top violations found during inspections include **limits on hours of work** (directly scheduling-related), public holiday pay, and vacation pay.

**Retail is a targeted sector.** The Ministry of Labour has conducted enforcement blitzes specifically focused on retail, noting that the sector *"often employs young, entry-level and part-time workers who may be at greater risk of having their rights violated."* In 2025, the MLITSD began proactive inspections of **retail and restaurant establishments in southwestern Ontario** (September–December 2025) due to a significant increase in workplace incidents.

### Three-hour rule (Section 21.2)

Under ESA Section 21.2(1), effective January 1, 2019: if an employee who regularly works more than three hours a day is required to present themselves for work but **works less than three hours** (despite being available), the employer must pay the greater of actual wages earned or **three hours at the employee's regular rate**. This applies to on-call employees called in, employees sent home early because business is slow (Monkhouse Law confirms *"being sent home early because the business is slow does not fall into any ESA exemption"*), and even short Saturday meetings for employees who normally work 8-hour days. Violations fall under the general ESA enforcement framework — penalties of $250–$5,000 per employee per contravention. Payment of wages violations are the **#1 category** found in claim investigations. Beyond the ESA penalty, significant reductions in scheduled hours can constitute **constructive dismissal** under common law, triggering severance obligations.

### Cost of an ESA investigation

Filing an ESA claim is **free for employees** — there is zero barrier to filing. A single ESA complaint conservatively costs a business **$5,000–$25,000+** in combined expenses: employment lawyers in Toronto charge **$300–$600+/hour**, initial consultations run $300–$500, and the investigation process takes **3–6 months** from filing to resolution. If the employer disagrees with the officer's decision and appeals to the OLRB, they must **deposit the full amount of the order in trust** before the appeal proceeds — and even if they win, they absorb their own legal costs (the OLRB cannot award costs). A **10% administrative fee** is added to all orders to pay, with no maximum limit on back wages since February 2015. Most critically, all **convictions are published on the government website** for one year — a reputational risk for a premium Yorkville retailer.

### Working for Workers Act requirements

Seven "Working for Workers" bills have been passed between 2021 and 2025, creating an expanding web of compliance obligations:

- **Electronic monitoring disclosure** (Bill 88, effective October 11, 2022): Employers with 25+ employees must maintain a written policy stating whether, how, and why they monitor employees electronically — including electronic time-clock systems, GPS tracking, and scanning sensors. Must be provided to all employees within 30 days of preparation and to new hires within 30 days of start date. Non-compliance triggers standard ESA penalties ($250–$5,000 per employee per contravention, up to $100,000 prosecution). **Directly relevant:** any scheduling app that tracks employee location, time, or activity constitutes electronic monitoring requiring this policy.
- **Right to disconnect** (Bill 27, effective January 1, 2022): Employers with 25+ employees must have a written disconnect policy. While this doesn't create an enforceable right, it creates **documented expectations** about after-hours contact. A scheduling app sending shift notifications at all hours could create scrutiny.
- **Mandatory employment information for new hires** (O. Reg. 477/24, effective **July 1, 2025**): Employers with 25+ employees must provide new staff in writing — before their first day — with expected hours of work, pay period, pay date, and other terms. A scheduling system directly supports this compliance.
- **Pay transparency in job postings** (effective **January 1, 2026**): Must include expected compensation range (max range $50,000).
- **Long-term illness leave** (effective **June 19, 2025**): Up to 27 weeks unpaid leave — creates scheduling complexity.

**No enforcement actions** specifically for electronic monitoring or disconnect policy violations have been publicly identified yet — but the enforcement framework is active and the regulatory trajectory over 7 bills in 4 years signals continued expansion. Ontario does **not yet** have a predictive scheduling law (Bill 148's scheduling provisions were repealed in 2018 by Bill 47), but the legislative trend strongly suggests future scheduling-specific regulation.

### Top 3 data points for Slide 7

1. **$562,500** — largest single ESA fine in 2024, imposed on a single business (11997629 Canada Ltd, February 2024)
2. **11,940 investigations and 111 prosecutions** in 2024–25 — prosecutions have tripled in two years, with retail as a specifically targeted sector
3. **$5,000 per affected employee** — the new third-contravention penalty rate (May 2024), meaning a single compliance failure affecting 40 employees = **$200,000**

## 4. Why custom software beats SaaS for small retail (Slide 9)

### SaaS pricing is escalating rapidly

SaaS pricing rose **11.4% in early 2025** compared to the same period in 2024 — nearly 5× the G7 average inflation rate of 2.7% (SaaStr, 2025). The Vertice SaaS Inflation Index shows per-employee SaaS costs reached **$9,100 by end of 2025**, up from $7,900 in 2023 — a 15% increase in two years. **73% of SaaS vendors** increased prices since August 2022, with an average 12% hike (CFO Dive, 2023). A growing trend is "AI bundling" — vendors forcing **15–20% price increases** by packaging AI features into core plans whether customers use them or not (CloudNuro, 2026). Nearly **90% of software companies** raised or planned to raise prices in 2024 (Blue Ridge Partners CXO Survey).

The most telling consolidation example is the **Kronos + Ultimate Software merger into UKG** ($22 billion, April 2020). Post-merger, UKG pricing sits at **$27–37 per employee per month** plus one-time implementation fees of 40–70% of annual software fees. UKG laid off **2,200 employees in summer 2024**, and reviews on Vendr report *"pre-set auto-renewals that sales reps have little to no flexibility to get out of"* and that UKG *"will not negotiate on renewals."* ShiftPlanning was acquired by TCP Software in 2020, rebranded to Humanity, and pricing became quote-only — opaque and uncontrollable.

### Deputy, When I Work, and 7shifts each have material problems

**Deputy** (Australian company, $6/user/month for Premium) admitted in response to a Trustpilot complaint that its auto-scheduling *"does not ensure that employees are scheduled exclusively when marked as available, nor does it automatically identify conflicts."* Reviews cite customer support degradation (*"Customer services is absolutely disgraceful these days"* — Capterra, April 2025), frequent app crashes, inconsistent PTO/leave accrual, and charges for unused services. As an Australian company using AWS, employee data may reside in US, UK, or Australian regions — **not Canada**.

**When I Work** (US company, Minneapolis, $4–10/user/month) has **no confirmed security certifications** (missing ISO 27001, SOC-2 per Connecteam analysis), no compliance support for Canadian provincial labor laws (no ESA or provincial regulation features documented), and data is stored on AWS presumably in the US — **directly subject to the CLOUD Act**. Reviews note costs escalate quickly with add-on features and no refund policy.

**7shifts** (Canadian company, Saskatoon, per-location pricing starting at $34.99/month) is **explicitly designed for restaurants, not retail**. A SelectSoftware reviewer stated: *"It is not a good fit for organizations that require comprehensive employee management solutions, such as those in retail or businesses that carry inventory."* Features like tip pooling, POS integration (Toast), and menu-item tracking are irrelevant to a denim retailer. A G2 reviewer noted *"Nothing new has been relevant in Canada, tip pooling and such is restricted to the US."* Glassdoor reviews from 2024–2025 raise stability concerns: *"How does a company raise 80 million dollars and proceed to have 3 rounds of layoffs within two years?"*

### Data sovereignty is a real and growing concern

**67% of SaaS tools used in Canada are US-owned and CLOUD Act-exposed** (Upper Harbour, 2026). The Canadian government's own white paper states: *"As long as a CSP that operates in Canada is subject to the laws of a foreign country, Canada will not have full sovereignty over its data."* Over **80% of Canadian businesses** use foreign-owned cloud providers — flagged as a *"major economic and national security vulnerability"* by the Dais think tank at Toronto Metropolitan University.

The **CLOUD Act** (2018) allows US federal law enforcement to compel US-based tech companies to produce data **regardless of where it is physically stored** — including data on servers in Canada. A Microsoft executive testified before the French Senate in June 2025 that he **could not guarantee** government data would not be transmitted to US authorities. The **Supreme Court of Canada** in R v Spencer (2014) and R v Bykovets (2024) explicitly rejected the US "third-party doctrine" — Canadians retain reasonable privacy expectations in electronic data that US law does not recognize.

Employee scheduling data includes names, SINs (via payroll integration), addresses, availability patterns, location data, and work hours — all sensitive personal information. Storing it on US-controlled platforms creates an irreconcilable conflict between US legal compulsion and Canadian privacy obligations.

### Contract lock-in traps are endemic to SaaS

Most SaaS contracts auto-renew unless cancelled 30–90 days before expiry. Miss the window by one day and you're locked in for another full year. Industry analysis estimates the effective **"exit tax" at 150–200% of Annual Contract Value** when factoring data migration, retraining, and lost productivity (CloudNuro). Proprietary data formats make export technically difficult; some contracts charge exorbitant fees to export your own data. UKG specifically is described as having auto-renewals that *"sales reps have little to no flexibility to get out of."*

### Total cost of ownership comparison (40-person store, 1 location)

| Solution | Year 1 | 3-Year TCO | 5-Year TCO (with ~10% annual inflation) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Deputy Premium** ($6/user/mo) | $2,880 | $8,640 | ~$17,600 |

| **Custom-built** (est.) | $30,000–$50,000 initial | $38,400–$72,000 | $46,800–$88,000 |

For a single location, SaaS is cheaper on pure sticker price. The custom value proposition rests on five factors beyond direct cost: (1) **no per-user fees** — cost stays flat as the team grows; (2) **Canadian data sovereignty** — data stored exclusively on Canadian servers under Canadian law; (3) **built-in Ontario ESA compliance** — three-hour rule, statutory holidays, electronic monitoring, expected hours — rather than generic US-centric features; (4) **no vendor lock-in** — the business owns the code, data, and future; (5) **no surprise price hikes** — predictable maintenance costs. The custom solution also becomes cost-competitive at **multiple locations or 50+ employees**, and when SaaS price escalation of 10%+ annually is factored in. A Swiss industrial case study (Edana, 2025) showed a 500-user custom solution **reduced TCO by 70%** versus SaaS over 5 years.

3. **67% of SaaS tools in Canada are CLOUD Act-exposed** — US law enforcement can compel data disclosure regardless of where servers sit

## 5. Employee scheduling and retail workforce stats (general deck enrichment)

### Mobile schedule access and job satisfaction

**83% of hourly employees** prefer accessing schedules via mobile devices (myshyft.com industry benchmarks). **67% of service industry employees** said they would be "extremely likely" to use a mobile scheduling app (Software Advice). Push notifications via mobile scheduling apps receive **4.8× higher response rates** than email for schedule changes. **76% of mobile scheduling actions** — shift checking, availability updates, swap requests — occur **outside traditional work hours**, underscoring the need for 24/7 digital access. The Gallup/American Job Quality Study (18,000+ U.S. workers, 2024–2025) found that employees with low-quality schedules report **~1 full point lower** job satisfaction on a 0–10 scale. **62% of U.S. employees** do not have high-quality work schedules. Legion Workforce Management customers saw employee schedule satisfaction **jump 53%** after implementation.

### Schedule predictability reduces absenteeism

The landmark **Gap Inc. Stable Scheduling Study** (Kesavan, Lambert, Williams & Pendem, published in *Management Science*, 2022) — a rigorous field experiment at 28 Gap stores analyzing 150,000+ shifts of 1,500 employees — found that predictable scheduling practices produced a **5.1% increase in store productivity** (sales per labor hour), a **3.3% increase in sales**, **1.8% reduction in labor hours**, and **median sales rose 7%** in treatment stores. Treatment stores generated an additional **$6.20 of revenue per hour of labor**. A Harvard Business School working paper analyzing **28 million time cards** across 1,000+ stores found that **37% of shifts** had inconsistent schedules (changing days or start times by >1 hour), and in those cases **16% of workers were late or absent**. Companies implementing predictable scheduling see an average **30% decrease in absenteeism** (aggregated research via myshyft.com). The **Seattle Fair Workweek study** (Harknett & Schneider, published in *PNAS*, 2021) showed that predictable scheduling legislation produced a **10 percentage-point decrease** in material hardships among workers, along with improved sleep quality and economic security.

### Canadian retail turnover

The **Mercer 2024 Canadian Turnover Survey** (1,146 organizations) — the most authoritative Canadian source — found Canadian retail & wholesale turnover at **25.9%**, the **highest of any industry** and more than **double the national average of 11.9%**. Ontario's retail trade sector employs **841,500 people** (10.3% of the province's workforce), with **27.5% aged 15–24** and **35.5% working part-time** — a demographic profile that amplifies scheduling sensitivity. Average hourly wage in Ontario retail is **$23.27** versus $30.50 provincial average. An **Altrum/Retail Insider report** (February 2026) found **42% of retail sales associates** are considering leaving due to low wages, volatile schedules, and lack of growth. Canadian retail **lost 28,000+ jobs in March 2025 alone**, and only **47.9% of recruitment targets** were met in 2024.

### Cost of a no-show shift

A single no-show in a small retail store costs **$300–$500** combining overtime ($50–$150), lost sales ($200–$400), and manager time (2–3 hours finding coverage) per Homebase. At one no-show per week, the annual cost reaches **$15,000–$25,000** — enough to hire another part-timer. The **CDC Foundation** reports absenteeism costs U.S. employers **$1,685 per employee per year**. SHRM data shows replacement workers are typically **~30% less productive** than the absent employee.

### Shift swap adoption: digital vs. manual

## 6. Data privacy for Canadian businesses (Slide 8)

### Real data breaches at scheduling and HR SaaS platforms

The **Kronos/UKG ransomware attack** (December 11, 2021) is the definitive cautionary tale. The attack disabled UKG's Kronos Private Cloud platform, affecting approximately **8,000 institutions and 8 million employees** — including FedEx, PepsiCo, Whole Foods, Tesla, Gap, Samsung, and the New York MTA. Systems were **down for weeks** during the holiday season, with full restoration not achieved until well into January 2022. Employers could not process payroll, access timesheets, or manage schedules, forcing many back to **manual paper-based processes**. Data compromised included names, addresses, Social Security numbers, and employee IDs. Total legal settlements exceeded **$9.6 million** — including a **$6 million class action settlement** (UKG), **$2.4 million from Cargill** for unpaid wages, and **$1.2 million from UMass Memorial Health** for FLSA violations. The breach likely exploited the **Log4Shell zero-day vulnerability**. The **ADP data breach** (2016) saw identity thieves exploit ADP's self-service portal to steal W-2 tax and salary data from at least **1,280 employees of U.S. Bancorp** alone — ADP serves 640,000+ companies worldwide. No major publicized breaches have been found for Deputy, When I Work, or 7shifts specifically.

### PIPEDA enforcement on cross-border employee data

PIPEDA **does not prohibit** cross-border data transfers but imposes strict **accountability obligations**. The OPC's 2009 Guidelines for Processing Personal Data Across Borders require organizations to: (1) remain **fully accountable** for transferred data, (2) **be transparent** that data may be sent to another jurisdiction, (3) inform individuals that foreign courts and law enforcement may access it, and (4) implement **contractual safeguards** including risk assessments and vendor audits.

**OPC Report of Findings #2020-001** (August 2020) — the key precedent — involved a Canadian financial institution transferring employee data to a service provider in India. The OPC found the transfer did not violate PIPEDA, but made clear that **accountability cannot be contracted away** — the originating Canadian organization bears full responsibility for any vendor's security failure. The OPC has also found that **always-on audio recording** of employees (Findings #2021-008 and #2022-006) was "unreasonable" under PIPEDA, establishing that employee monitoring must be **proportionate** — directly relevant to scheduling apps that track location or activity.

**Critical gap for Ontario retailers:** PIPEDA generally does **not** cover employee data for provincially-regulated employers in Ontario. This means a Toronto retailer's employee scheduling data falls into a **regulatory gap** — insufficient provincial protection while still being exposed to foreign law enforcement access when stored on US platforms. However, when data **crosses international borders** (as it does with US-hosted SaaS), PIPEDA does apply to those transfers.

### Upcoming privacy legislation creates urgency

Federal **Bill C-27** (Digital Charter Implementation Act) — which would have replaced PIPEDA with the Consumer Privacy Protection Act — **died on the order paper** when Parliament was prorogued in January 2025. A new federal privacy statute expected in **late 2025 or early 2026** is anticipated to include penalties of **up to C$25 million or 5% of gross global revenue** and **data sovereignty measures** (Osler, Hoskin & Harcourt, 2026 Privacy Priorities). Ontario's June 2021 white paper, "Modernizing Privacy in Ontario," proposed Ontario's first comprehensive private-sector privacy law that would — critically — **cover employee personal information** of provincially-regulated businesses. The white paper proposed rights including a right to be forgotten, automated decision-making transparency, and data portability. While Ontario has not yet tabled this legislation, the trajectory is clear.

**Quebec's Law 25** (fully effective September 2024) provides the benchmark. Penalties reach **C$25 million or 4% of worldwide turnover** for penal fines, with a **C$1,000 minimum statutory damages** per person in private right of action. Law 25 requires a **mandatory Privacy Impact Assessment** before disclosing personal information outside Quebec and verification that the recipient jurisdiction provides equivalent protections. The Quebec CAI received **444 confidentiality incident declarations** in 2023–2024 alone. Any retailer with Quebec customers or employees is already subject to Law 25.

### US law exposure for Canadian data

The **CLOUD Act** (2018) allows US federal law enforcement to compel US-based tech companies to produce data **regardless of where it is stored** — including data on Canadian servers. There is **no bilateral CLOUD Act agreement between Canada and the US** as of 2026. The **PATRIOT Act** grants US agencies power to issue National Security Letters requiring data disclosure **without informing affected parties** and sometimes without judicial oversight — applicable to foreign subsidiaries of American companies. The Canadian government's **own federal policy** expressly forbids storing sensitive government data on US servers. **Over 25%** of purely domestic Canadian internet traffic takes a "boomerang" route through US routers, exposing it to NSA surveillance. Osler's 2025 analysis acknowledges that while there is *"no evidence to suggest"* foreign access happens frequently, the **legal authority exists** and the risk is non-zero — and a new federal privacy statute is expected to incorporate data sovereignty measures.

The **March 2026 OPC findings against Loblaw** (#2026-001, 17+ million PC Optimum members) — for failure to properly delete data on request and retention of re-identifiable data — demonstrate the OPC's growing enforcement appetite and establish strict standards for data deletion and anonymization that apply equally to employee data.

1. **8,000 businesses and 8 million employees disrupted** by the Kronos/UKG ransomware attack — with $9.6M+ in legal settlements and weeks of system downtime during holiday season
2. **C$25 million or 5% of gross global revenue** — expected penalty ceiling in upcoming federal privacy legislation, with data sovereignty measures anticipated
3. **Ontario's employee data regulatory gap** — no comprehensive provincial privacy law covers provincially-regulated employee data, yet the white paper signals this will change, making proactive Canadian-hosted data storage a future-proof investment
